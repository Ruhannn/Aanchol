AANCHOL

An AI shelter so no child slips through the net.

1. The problem we're tackling

Bangladesh is genuinely good at vaccinating children. Since the Expanded Programme on Immunization (EPI) started in 1979, full immunization has climbed from 2% to 81.6% (Coverage Evaluation Survey, 2023). The trouble is the children left over. Around 400,000 are only partly vaccinated, and roughly 70,000 have never had a single dose. These children aren't spread evenly either: coverage in cities sits at 79%, below the 85% in rural areas, and the worst gaps are in slums and hard-to-reach unions [1], [2].

When we looked at why, the same handful of reasons kept turning up. Parents lose track of a schedule that runs across many visits, and it's usually the later doses that get skipped. Babies born at home often never get registered anywhere, so on paper they don't exist. And some families simply can't reach a centre — it might be 20 km away, or the road is gone after a flood.

Here is the part that's easy to miss: Bangladesh has already built the database. VaxEPI, launched by the DGHS in 2024, registers every child against their birth registration number and logs each dose [4]. So the real problem isn't "no records" anymore. It's that a record sitting in a database doesn't do anything by itself. It can't warn you that a child is about to miss a dose, find the baby who was never entered, or call a mother who has no smartphone. That is the gap Aanchol is built to close.

2. What Aanchol does

Aanchol takes the data the government already collects and actually acts on it. The name comes from the aanchol, the corner of a mother's sari she uses to cover her baby — which is more or less the job here: keep a protective layer over every child so none gets missed.

At the centre is a prediction model, built on Google Vertex AI, that looks at a few things we already know about each child — their age, which dose comes next, how far they live from a centre, and where they are — and works out three things:

  • how likely the child is to miss the next dose;
  • whether an area is likely to hold unregistered, never-vaccinated children; and
  • whether a child is effectively cut off, by distance or by a flood or cyclone.

A prediction is useless if it just sits on a screen, so the other half of Aanchol is about reaching the family — in a way that actually works here. Many parents don't own a smartphone, and rural clinics have patchy internet, so the system runs offline and uses whatever channel fits. For most families that's an automated voice call in Bangla (no reading needed, works on any phone) or an SMS. For tougher cases, a health worker gets the child added to their visit list, or we reach the family through the bKash/Nagad agents who are already in every neighbourhood. And when a child is stranded by distance or disaster, the system sends a mobile team to their actual location using Google Maps.

All of this feeds a live dashboard (Looker Studio) so EPI managers can see coverage ward by ward and act on it. Gemini runs the Bangla voice and a helpline parents can call; Firebase keeps the worker app working offline; Document AI scans the old paper cards into the system.

3. How it pays for itself

We're firm on one point: parents never pay. Charging poor families to get their children vaccinated would be wrong, and it wouldn't work anyway. The money comes from elsewhere. The apps for parents and health workers stay free. The core system is meant to run as part of the government's own health infrastructure, funded the way VaxEPI already is. The actual revenue comes from a paid analytics layer — the dashboards, reports and targeting tools — which city corporations, NGOs and partners like Gavi, UNICEF and WHO would pay for, since reaching zero-dose children is already where they spend. Immunization returns about US$25 for every US$1 put in [3]; Aanchol simply helps aim that spending better.

4. Why it matters

The children Aanchol reaches for first are exactly the ones the current system tends to lose: kids in slums, kids who were never registered, kids in a district a flood just cut off. By speaking to parents in their own language, on the phone they actually have, and by sending help to the child instead of asking a mother to make the trip, it reaches the people an app-and-smartphone approach quietly leaves out. Reaching even a share of the roughly 470,000 under- and unvaccinated children means real illnesses prevented and real lives saved.
